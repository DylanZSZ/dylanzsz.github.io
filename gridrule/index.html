Interactive ARC-AGI-3-style environment · label-free RL · ≤1.5B scale

Can a tiny model learn to decompose problems
by proposing its own subgoals?

We built GridRule — a cheap, procedural gridworld capturing ARC-AGI-3's two hard demands: latent-rule induction by trial and error, and compose-back subgoal chaining. We then asked whether a 0.8B model trained with label-free self-distillation can learn a transferable decomposition skill by proposing the subgoals it wants to reach — nothing else.

A note before you read

This is a pilot endeavor — small-scale, exploratory, single-researcher. Most claims rest on n=2–3 seeds; the environment is synthetic. We share this honestly as an early conversation starter. Treat the findings as encouraging and preliminary, not conclusive.

TL;DR — pilot findings
  • The env is the contribution as much as the results. GridRule has explicit knobs to isolate two sources of difficulty — rule discovery and subgoal chaining — and an oracle-execution probe that lets you separate planning failures from navigation failures.
  • Self-proposed coordinate subgoals produce a transferable decomposition skill. Train on single-gate rooms, test on three-gate chains — the planning transfers. Replicated on two independent seeds, ≈1.8× baseline pass@1.
  • The bottleneck is execution, not decomposition. Give the model perfect navigation to its own proposed waypoints and unseen 3-gate tasks jump from 0.26 → 0.80+. The model knows where to go; it struggles to get there over long chains.
  • Different mechanics break different faculties. Irreversibility breaks planning (oracle-exec provides zero rescue). Hazards break navigation. Crafting breaks sequencing. The env can tell these apart cleanly.
The environment

GridRule: a diagnostic research instrument

Multi-step problem solving usually bundles two distinct sources of difficulty together: you don't know the rules, and winning requires chaining sub-plans where an early mistake strands you. I wanted to pull those apart and measure them independently. That's the design goal behind GridRule.

The env has explicit knobs. You can reveal or hide controls (which action moves which direction), object roles (which glyph means key vs decoy), and key-door bindings — independently, per episode. Turning them on and off lets you isolate exactly which source of difficulty the model is struggling with.

Compose-back: errors propagate

The chain structure is the key design idea. To win, you must get the key, open the door, hit the switch, open the gate — in order. It's not just "navigate to a goal." A wrong early subgoal strands you with no way forward. This means sub-policies genuinely depend on each other, and a model that gets the chain half-right still fails.

The reward penalizes waste too: efficiency = (optimal steps / steps used)². Trial-and-error bumping is allowed but costly. The model learns to be right and efficient.

The method

CTTD + self-proposed subgoals

The training loop is CTTD (Continual Test-Time self-Distillation): a four-phase cycle that lets the model teach itself from its own successful episodes — no human labels anywhere.

  • W
    WAKE

    Roll out K episodes per level. The model emits SUBGOAL:(r,c) at each turn, then acts. The environment verifies whether the proposed cell was reached.

  • R
    REWARD

    Score each episode with environment success and action efficiency (RHAE = min(1, optimal/used)²). No oracle labels — the only signal is whether the model won, and how efficiently.

  • F
    FILTER

    Keep only efficient successes (≤2.5× optimal). Optionally cap duplicate behavioral signatures (--diverse-filter) so one lucky trajectory can't flood the distillation set.

  • D
    DREAM

    LoRA-SFT on kept episodes + replayed buffer. EMA weight averaging (α=0.7) smooths the policy. Advance to next stage when the promotion bar is met on a held-out eval set.

Hindsight distillation

If an episode fails overall but the model did reach its proposed subgoal, we distill the prefix up to subgoal-achievement. This recycles "partial credit" episodes — the model learns this decomposition step was correct even if the rest went wrong.

Why coordinate subgoals? Typed abstract labels (ITEM / GATE / EXIT) gave ~0 bootstrapping at 0.8B — abstract names provide no navigation anchor. Emitting a coordinate forces the model to localize a target, doing double duty as decomposition and perception anchor.

Mechanism

Decomposition transfers — execution is the bottleneck

To separate planning from navigation, we built an oracle-execution probe: the environment auto-navigates (via BFS) to whatever subgoal the model proposes. The model only plans; execution is perfect.

StageNormal pass@1Oracle-exec pass@1Lift
s0 1-door0.7381.000+0.26
s1 1-gate0.6940.950+0.26
s3 2-door0.7440.950+0.21
s6 3-gate (unseen)0.2630.80+0.54
Replicated on two seeds

On s6 (3-gate, never trained on): seed-2 → 0.26→0.80, seed-3 → 0.38→0.95. The model's decomposition for unseen 3-gate tasks is near-perfect. What collapses performance is navigating there over long chains — execution failure, not planning failure.

Transfers well ✓

Decomposition — which subgoal to pursue next. Proposal sensibility is 2.5–5× above chance on every unseen stage; doesn't degrade at 3-gate depth.

Execution bottleneck ✗

Multi-step navigation to the waypoint. More cells, more steps, more failure opportunities. Bigger models help here (+0.11 at 1.7B vs 0.8B on the hardest stage).

Three cheap compound gains

LoRA-souping, diverse filtering, capacity

LoRA-souping: eliminate seed selection, for free

Rather than cherry-picking the best seed post-hoc, we average the LoRA weights of 3 trained adapters (model souping) and evaluate the averaged model — no new training.

The soup scores 0.506 on unseen s1–s4, above the best individual seed (0.491) and the 3-seed mean (0.440). Eliminates the seed-selection problem and lifts transfer for free.

Extending the env (exploratory)

Each new mechanic targets a different faculty

One thing the knob-based design makes possible: you can add a new mechanic and ask precisely which faculty it stresses. We added six. Three were learnable; three exposed hard limits. The oracle-execution probe separates them cleanly.

The learnable ones

Fog (partial observation), drift (controls reshuffle every 6 steps), and AND-gates (need a full set of keys) all bootstrap well with a warm-started prior. The subgoal proposal recipe is robust to these stresses — the model adapts.

Mechanicpass@1What it stresses
⑤ Fog (partial obs, warm start)0.64exploration under hidden state — cold start stalls at 0.08
④ Drift (reshuffled controls)0.77non-stationarity — controls change mid-episode
③ AND-gates (set-valued keys)0.68richer prerequisites — collect all before you can proceed

The hard ones — and what they reveal

Irreversibility, crafting, and hazards all stall or fail to bootstrap. But the reason is different in each case. That's what makes them interesting as diagnostics.

The oracle-execution probe makes this visible. Hand the model perfect navigation to its own proposed waypoints. If performance recovers, the failure was navigational. If it doesn't, the plan itself was wrong.

MechanicNormalOracle-execDiagnosis
② Irreversibility0.340.33 (no rescue)Planning — model proposes the trap (16–22% of episodes)
E1 Crafting0.200.38Sequencing — model knows the ingredients, mis-orders them
E8 Hazards0.260.44Navigation — model steps onto lethal tiles; routing rescues it
Irreversibility flips the bottleneck

Under reversible mechanics, oracle-exec rescues nearly everything — 2-door goes 0.744 → 0.950. Under irreversibility on the same 1-door task, oracle-exec provides zero lift. The model is walking into a trap it chose. Perfect execution just gets it there faster. This is the clearest demonstration that the failure mode depends on the mechanic, not the model — and that the env can tell them apart.

Honest caveats

What this pilot can and cannot claim

  • !
    Small n.

    Most claims rest on n=2–3 seeds. The 1.8× headline is reproduced on two seeds, which is meaningful, but far from a rigorous statistical claim. The ceiling is still seed-variable.

  • !
    Synthetic environment.

    GridRule captures the demands we care about in a convenient package. We cannot claim the findings transfer to visual, high-dimensional, or open-ended settings.

  • !
    Frontier mechanics at n=1.

    The three-failure-mode taxonomy is a hypothesis to replicate, not an established result.

None of these caveats undermine the core finding: self-proposed coordinate subgoals, in this setting, reliably improve transfer and compositional generalization, replicated at n=2. The pilot is a starting point. We share it to invite scrutiny and collaboration.

Takeaways

What this pilot suggests

On the env

Knobs that isolate difficulty sources + an oracle-execution probe let you ask which faculty is binding, not just whether the model passes or fails.

On decomposition

Self-proposed coordinate subgoals produce a transferable planning skill — trained on 1-gate, generalizes to 3-gate. The planning transfers further than the navigation.

On failure modes

The failure mode is a property of the mechanic, not the model. Irreversibility breaks planning; hazards break navigation; crafting breaks sequencing. The env separates them.

The thing I find most interesting isn't the pass@1 numbers — it's that the env can tell the difference between a model that doesn't know where to go and a model that knows exactly where to go but can't get there. That distinction seems worth building tools around.
